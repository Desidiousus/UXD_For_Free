Who is going to be using this application?

I am the user.

Myself exclusively. 

This program's purpose is to generate documents for my client's consumption. 

I will enter client information into the front-end, post interview, via recorded transcription. The client will never see this application; just the generated site map, wire frame key legend, and Text outlines.

Read_Me.txt:

This Text file needs to communicate the following to Web Devs/Designers, Content Creators and back-end devs:

  1. Who this is for, and how to interpret this information.
    • The contents of this folder contains the blueprints for all collaborative parties involved in the creation of this website/ application.
      • Web Devs/  Designers/ Content Creators/ Company Stakeholders

    • How to use and view each file. (General overview)
      • All of these documents are in html format, and can be viewed on any browser/ desktop.
        • I chose the html format in order to generate dynamic, informative blueprints that expound on each piece of content's specifications/ motivations in one central location.
          • As of now these html documents are not mobile/ tablet friendly...
            • I recommend using the Chrome browsers
            • Remember the play with your zoom settings:
              • ctrl + '-' |  ctrl + '+' | ctrl + '0' (reset to 100% zoom)
          • They can be viewed the offline, so long as the file is not moved from the original folder.
            • (Due to their third party extensions)
  2. What is User Experience Design?"
    • User Experience Design, according to Jesse James Garrett, is comprised of these five layers starting from top to bottom:
      1. Surface
        • Visual design is essentially the look of the finished product. 
          • What does it look like? 
          • What fonts are we using? 
          • What colors are we using?
          • How does all of that reinforce and support the information structure? 
          • How does all of that reinforce and support our means of navigation of direction of feedback.
      2. Skeleton
        • Comprised of two elements:
          1. Interface Design is the arrangement of all the elements, within the user interface, that enables us
            to interact with the functionality of the application as a whole. (***Wire frames***)
          2. Navigation design is a critical critical element particularly in terms of good user experience because navigation determines how the user moves through the information.
      3. Structure
        • Comprised of two elements:
          1. Interaction Design - How does the system behave when somebody does something. How your user will typically, or ideally, navigate through your website? 
          2. Information Architecture - The creation of organizational and navigational schemes.
            1. Good IA allows people to intuitively navigate through content effectively, with little to no fatigue. 
              1. Effective IA is flexible, adaptive to changes, and accommodates growth. (***Site Maps***)
      4. Scope
        • Strategy becomes scope when user needs and business objectives are translated into requirements for content and functionality. A clear description of what you're going to build tells everyone involved what the project's goals are. With no documented requirements youre just playing telephone...
          1. Content Requirements
            • What's required to provide value to the users?
            • What information does the user need to know in order to conduct business with you responsibly? What are some headlines or ideas for content that your users would find valuable? 
            • Content should be user-centered, and have a major impact on User Experience
          2. Functional Requirements
            • What the user needs to be able to accomplish using your website/ application.
      5. Strategy
        • Comprised of two elements (both of which must have measurable success metrics):
          1. User needs 
            • Where are the things that people expect? 
            • What do they want when they use our product? 
            • What it is that the audience wants from us, and how that sort of fits in with their other goals?
          2. Business objectives 
            • Largely concerned with success metrics of some point.
  3. How UXD is communicated through these documents?
    • The documents in this folder cover the first 4 of the 5 Elements of User Experience Design:
      1. Strategy
        • User & Business "Objectives" are explicitly outlined.
          1. Based on market research, and company stakeholder objectives/ goals.
          2. Again, these goals have measurable success metrics.
      2. Scope
        • These are the "Requirements" that are needed to be met in order to achieve the objective upon which it is based.
          1. Content requirements
            • Definition of content elements required in the site in order to meet their respective user/ business objectives.
          2. Functional Specifications
            • Feature set , or detailed descriptions of functionality the site must include in order to meet their respective user/ business objectives.
      3. Structure
        • "Content/ Functional Deliverables":
          1. Defined to establish the agreed upon set of procedures to fulfill its respective requirement.
          2. These Deliverables are then categorized based on the web page in which they will be located.
          3. You can view these Deliverables via the Site_Map.html document.
            • Each Node represents its respective web page.
              • Clicking the node, opens a modal window listing out all of the deliverables to be displayed within its page.
                • Clicking any of the listed Deliverable titles within displays relevant information
                  • This includes info on the Deliverable itself, as well as the Requirement & Objective upon which it is based.
      4. Skeleton
        • ***This stage is entirely optional, and is usually reserved for complex website/ app page layouts.***
        • Stakeholders request Wire Frames for specified web/ app pages and display resolution. 
          • These Wire Frames are also interactable html documents, and serve to visually communicate the local placement of grouped & independent deliverables.
            • Red nodes indicate an independent Deliverable
              • Clicking this node opens a modal window displaying its deliverable (red), requirement (yellow), objective(green)
            • Light blue nodes indicate a grouping of Deliverables
              • Also, opens a modal window, but contains a list of its contained deliverables.
                • Clicking this node opens a modal window displaying its deliverable (red), requirement (yellow), objective(green) 
  4. How to use/ interpret the documents within this folder:
    • ObjReqDel_RowCol_View.html
      • This is a way to view...
        • The list of "Goals/ Objectives" defined & agreed upon by all invested stakeholders.
          • A list of their respective scope "Requirements".
            • And finally a list of "Deliverables", or the set of measures we will take to satisfy its respective Requirement.
      • Clicking on the Objective/ Requirement/ Deliverable title within the ObjReqDel_RowCol_View.html document will open a single window that points to it's page location within the TableOfContents.html. 
        • Both files must be in the same folder for this feature to work.
      • Toggle between row and column view via blue header button, located at the top of the ObjReqDel_RowCol_View.html document.
    • Site_Map.html
      • Sitemaps are a hierarchical diagrams outlining the structure of the website or application. They are used by User Experience Designers and Information Architects to define the taxonomy through grouping of related content. They are an important step of the user centered process as they ensure content is in places users would expect to find it. They can also be used as a reference point for wireframes, functional specifications and content maps. 
        • This document does communicate the structure of the website/ app, but has one small twist..
          • Each page node is a button that toggles a modal window that displays a list of the previously defined deliverables.
            • Local groups of deliverables are shown in blue, and toggle a drop-down of its contained deliverables (light blue).
              • Clicking any of the contained deliverables toggles their Deliverable(light blue) > Requirement(yellow) > Objective (green) information
              • Clicking the "/Return to/.../" will take you back to the list of deliverables for that grouping.
            • Independent deliverables are in red and toggle their Deliverable(red) > Requirement(yellow) > Objective (green) information.
    • Wire_Frame_[page_title].html
      • Similar to the site map, this document has grouped (light-blue) and independent deliverable (red) nodes.
        • These nodes can be clicked to toggle a modal window, displaying either the independent deliverable's information or the grouping's list of contained deliverables.
  5. Contact information for troubleshooting support, or questions regarding a clients project
    • Should ask for a retainer for such support...

  2. What is User Experience Design?"
    • User Experience Design, according to Jesse James Garrett, is comprised of these five layers starting from top to bottom:
      1. Surface
        • Visual design is essentially the look of the finished product. 
          • What does it look like? 
          • What fonts are we using? 
          • What colors are we using?
          • How does all of that reinforce and support the information structure? 
          • How does all of that reinforce and support our means of navigation of direction of feedback.
      2. Skeleton
        • Comprised of two elements:
          1. Interface Design is the arrangement of all the elements, within the user interface, that enables us
            to interact with the functionality of the application as a whole. (***Wire frames***)
          2. Navigation design is a critical critical element particularly in terms of good user experience because navigation determines how the user moves through the information.
      3. Structure
        • Comprised of two elements:
          1. Interaction Design - How does the system behave when somebody does something. How your user will typically, or ideally, navigate through your website? 
          2. Information Architecture - The creation of organizational and navigational schemes.
            1. Good IA allows people to intuitively navigate through content effectively, with little to no fatigue. 
              1. Effective IA is flexible, adaptive to changes, and accommodates growth. (***Site Maps***)
      4. Scope
        • Strategy becomes scope when user needs and business objectives are translated into requirements for content and functionality. A clear description of what you're going to build tells everyone involved what the project's goals are. With no documented requirements youre just playing telephone...
          1. Content Requirements
            • What's required to provide value to the users?
            • What information does the user need to know in order to conduct business with you responsibly? What are some headlines or ideas for content that your users would find valuable? 
            • Content should be user-centered, and have a major impact on User Experience
          2. Functional Requirements
            • What the user needs to be able to accomplish using your website/ application.
      5. Strategy
        • Comprised of two elements (both of which must have measurable success metrics):
          1. User needs 
            • Where are the things that people expect? 
            • What do they want when they use our product? 
            • What it is that the audience wants from us, and how that sort of fits in with their other goals?
          2. Business objectives 
            • Largely concerned with success metrics of some point.
